# Price Recommendation Audit: Espresso (Coffee Shop Demo)

> Traced end-to-end using demo data (1,400 transactions, 18 products, ~6-month span).

---

## Input Data

- **CSV**: Programmatically generated by `backend/engine/demo.py` (seed = 42, reproducible)
- **Transactions for Espresso**: ~120–180 estimated (18 products, 1,400 total ≈ 78 avg, Espresso is high-volume)
- **Date range**: ~6 months (today ± 180 days)
- **Price range**: Likely $3.00–$4.50 (coffee shop demo prices)
- **Key unknown**: Does the demo inject price variation per transaction, or are prices fixed?  
  - **If fixed prices**: coefficient of variation (CV) < 3% → elasticity estimation FAILS for all products  
  - **If variable** (modifiers, discounts, size variants): CV may exceed 3%, enabling elasticity

---

## Step 1: Does Espresso Qualify for a "Raise" Recommendation?

**From `pricing.py::_get_price_recommendations()`:**

```
RAISE triggers when:
  qty >= 65th percentile (across products)     AND
  price <= 35th percentile (across products)   AND
  n_transactions >= 25
```

For Espresso to trigger:
- It must be in the **top 35% of volume** across all 18 products — plausible for a flagship espresso
- It must be **priced below the portfolio median** — e.g., $3.50 espresso among $8–$15 food items
- It must have **25+ transactions** — easily satisfied at 120–180 txns

**Conclusion:** Espresso very likely qualifies for a RAISE recommendation.

## Step 2: Elasticity Estimation

**From `pricing.py::_estimate_product_elasticity()`:**

```python
# Gate 1: 10+ transactions — PASS (≥120)
# Gate 2: 5+ days of data — PASS (6 months)
# Gate 3: price CV > 3% — CRITICAL CHECK
```

### The Fixed-Price Problem

In most real-world POS exports, menu prices are fixed. A coffee shop sells Espresso at $3.50 consistently. The only price variation comes from:
- Size/modifier differences lumped under the same SKU name
- Occasional discounts or comps
- Manual price overrides

**If CV < 3%:** Elasticity returns `None` with message:
> "not enough price variation in the data to estimate reliably — prices appear fixed."

**If CV ≥ 3%:** The OLS regression runs with binned data:
- n_bins = min(10, n_days // 3) — with 180 days → min(10, 60) = 10 bins
- Bins require ≥ 2 observations each
- Minimum 5 bins after filtering
- Statistical gate: t_stat > max(1.8, 1.645 + 2.0/dof) AND R² > 0.20
- Elasticity capped at 2.5

**Realistic outcome:** For a coffee shop with fixed menu prices → **elasticity estimation fails** → confidence = "insufficient"

## Step 3: Recommendation Decision

**From `action_center.py::_build_action_center()`:**

```python
revenue_gain = monthly_qty × (suggested - current)
impact = revenue_gain × margin_pct   # margin_pct = actual cost margin OR 0.65 fallback
```

| Variable | Source | Data-driven? |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| `monthly_qty` | Actual txns ÷ months | ✅ Real data |
| `current` price | Actual avg revenue ÷ qty | ✅ Real data |
| `suggested` price | `current × 1.05` — hardcoded | ❌ Hardcoded +5% always |
| `margin_pct` | Cost data or **0.65 fallback** | ⚠️ Fallback never disclosed |
| Demand adjustment | **Not applied in impact calc** | ❌ Assumes zero volume loss |

**Example calculation (no cost data uploaded):**

```
monthly_qty = 150 units/month
current = $3.50
suggested = $3.68 (+5%)
revenue_gain = 150 × ($3.68 - $3.50) = $27.00
impact = $27.00 × 0.65 = $17.55/month
impact_low = $17.55 × 0.75 = $13.16
impact_high = $17.55 × 1.25 = $21.94
```

**What's shown to user:** "Could add ~$18/month" ← this looks like solid analysis.  
**What's hidden:** The 65% margin is an assumption, and zero demand loss is assumed.

## Elasticity Calculation (if data exists)

- **Log-log OLS coefficient**: Only calculable if price CV > 3%
- **R² threshold**: Must exceed 0.20 (fairly low bar)
- **t-statistic threshold**: `max(1.8, 1.645 + 2.0/dof)` — lenient for small datasets
- **Min transactions required**: 10 ✅ (espresso easily passes)
- **Min price variation required**: CV > 3% ⚠️ (may fail for fixed-price products)

## Recommendation Decision Logic

| Condition | Value | Data-driven? |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| Raise decision trigger | Qty ≥ 65th %ile AND price ≤ 35th %ile | ✅ Percentile of actual data |
| Price increase amount | Always +5% | ❌ Hardcoded |
| Impact calculation | monthly_qty × $0.18 × 0.65 | ⚠️ Hybrid |
| Confidence label | "insufficient" if no elasticity | ✅ Honest |
| Demand adjustment in impact | NOT APPLIED | ❌ Optimistic |

---

## Is This Data-Driven or Generic?

- ✅ **Custom**: Which product gets a RAISE is based on actual relative performance (percentile comparison across this business's products)
- ✅ **Custom**: Elasticity label (when calculable) comes from OLS on this product's history
- ⚠️ **Hybrid**: The impact dollar amount uses actual quantity but a hardcoded 5% increase and 65% fallback margin
- ❌ **Generic**: The "+5% suggestion" is identical for every product. A product with 500 txns gets the same +5% as one with 25 txns.
- ❌ **Opaque**: The 65% margin assumption is NEVER surfaced to the user

---

## Risk: Overfitting or Underfitting?

### Overfitting risk
- **Transactions**: ~120–180 — Low risk for the RAISE decision
- **Price variation**: If CV < 3% (fixed prices), elasticity defaults to "insufficient" — honest but unhelpful
- **If elasticity IS estimated on sparse bins**: 5 bins with 2 observations each is very little data for a regression

### Underfitting risk
- **Seasonality**: Not considered in pricing (6-month demo data would show seasonal patterns)
- **Day-of-week effects**: Not considered (coffee shops have strong Mon–Fri vs weekend patterns)
- **Promotional periods**: Not excluded from price data (promos could corrupt price/demand relationship)

## What a Customer Will Ask

**"Why raise the price? What if I lose customers?"**
> Answer when elasticity available: "Based on how your customers have responded to past price changes, a 5% increase will likely reduce volume by X%. The net effect is +$18/month."
> Answer when elasticity unavailable (most likely): "We don't have enough price variation in your data to estimate demand response. Run a 2-week test first."
> **Problem**: Most fixed-price businesses will always get the "test first" answer, making the recommendation feel weak.

**"How confident are you?"**
> Shown as: "Worth testing" (directional) or "Need more data" (insufficient)
> Not shown: n_transactions, R², p-value, price CV

**"What if I don't do this?"**
> Not shown: No opportunity cost calculation for the "do nothing" path

## Summary: Confidence Assessment

| Component | Honestly Data-Driven? | Risk |
|-----------|----------------------|------|
| Decision to raise | ✅ Yes (percentile logic) | Low |
| +5% amount | ❌ No (hardcoded) | Medium |
| Dollar impact | ⚠️ Hybrid (qty real, margin assumed) | High if customer asks about it |
| Elasticity sensitivity | ✅ Yes (when estimable) | Will fail for fixed-price businesses |
| Confidence tier | ✅ Yes (honestly "insufficient" when data thin) | Low |

**Bottom line:** The recommendation is data-driven in WHO it targets (high volume, low price products). It's NOT data-driven in HOW MUCH to raise or WHAT the actual impact will be. A savvy customer asking "show me the math" will hit the 65% margin assumption and hardcoded +5% quickly.
